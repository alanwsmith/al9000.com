The answer to the first question isn't entirely in your control. The answer to the second one is. This post is about making sure you can answer yes. ## You Will Lose If You Do Not Play There's a quote from The Wire that sticks in my head: > The game is rigged, but you cannot lose if you do not play. That makes sense, assuming it's a game you can opt-out of. With money, there's no way out. You're in the game whether you want to be or not. You can try to sit on the bench, but you're pretty much fucked if you do. There's good news, though. The path to victory isn't complicated. ## The Formula You only need to do three things to win at money: 1. Spend less than you make. 2. Invest what you don't spend in the [Vanguard Total Stock Market fund](https://investor.vanguard.com/investment-products/etfs/profile/vti). 3. Wait. The more you do of the first two, the less of the last one is required. ## What Winning Looks Like "Winning at money" is variable and open to interpretation. It's also not what I care about. My goal is different. I want control over my time. For me, winning at money is a means to an end. It's the point where I've got enough that the deicsisions about how I spend my time are up to me. ## The Fuck You Part Fuck You Money is the power to quit. That might mean quitting a job. "Fuck you, I'll be fine" is just as important. Covering you when someone else makes the decision to split ties. ## Timelines There's two levels of Fuck You Money: short term and long term. Short term Fuck You Money covers you during transition periods. A bridge between incomes. Long term Fuck You Money is the power to never again have to trade your time for money. Long term is the goal. Short term coverage happens naturally as you move towards it. ## The Compound Key My parents failed to teach me the most critical part about how money works: **Compound Interest** It's not their fault. They didn't get it either. ### An Example Of Bad Interest is the price paid to borrow money. For example, using a credit card is effectively boworring money. You don't get the loan directly. The money goes to whoever you're buying from, but you have to pay it back just like if you'd gotten a load from a bank. Say you've got a credit card that charges %25 at the end of every year but doesn't require you to make a payment for ten years. Now, let's say you charge $500 to it. As the first year rolls over, the 25% kicks in and the credit card company adds $125 to the bill. That $125 is the interest. At the end of the second year, the 25% is applied again. But, instead of $125, this time, the bill goes up $156 leaving you owing $781. That jump is because the interested isn't just calculated off the original $500. It get calculated of the original amount plus the $125 that was added on in the prior year. That's the compounding part. Every time the amount goes up, the calculation includues the amount from all the other times it's risen. The full ten year time line looks like this:

## Devilish Details Two things to notice about this table. 1. At year 8, the $596.05 interest charged is more than the initial $500 on the card. 2. Subtracting the initial $500 from the final total shows us that total interest charged is $4,156.61. That's four grand that just evaporates without getting anything else. The pattern is a lot harder to see in the day to day. Monthly payments are made, new charges are added, credit limits are reached. But, the math is always. Working against you as long as you're being charged interest. ## Turning Tables Compound interest is a tool. Banks and credit card companies use it to extract money from us.
