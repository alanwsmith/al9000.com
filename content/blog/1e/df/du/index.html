The answer to the first one isn't entirely in your control. The answer to the second one is. This post is about how to make sure the answer is yes. ## You Will Lose If You Do Not Play There's a quote from The Wire that sticks in my head: > The game is rigged, but you cannot lose if you do not play. That makes sense, if it's a game you can opt-out of. When participation isn't an option, trying not to play means losing. Managing money is one of those games. If you sit on the bench, if you don't play with intent, you're fucked. The good news: it doesn't take much to win. ## The One Two Punch You only need to do two things to win at money: 1. Spend less than you make. 2. Invest the rest. The time between startng and when you hit the finish line depends on how much you do those two things. Put enough into it, and you can leave the work world before you're body's broken. ## The Win Condition The term "winning at money" is open to interpretation. It's also secondary for me. My goal isn't actually about money. It's about time. Stated simply: I want control over how I spend my time. Money is just what gives me the power to do that. ## The Compound Key My parents failed to teach me the most critical part about how money works: **Compound Interest** It's not their fault. They didn't get it either. ## An Example Of Bad Interest is the price paid to borrow money. For example, using a credit card is effectively boworring money. You don't get the loan directly. The money goes to whoever you're buying from directly, but you have to pay it back just like if you'd gotten a load from a bank. Say you've got a credit card: - with $500 on it - that charges %25 at the end of every year - but doesn't require you to make a payment for ten years. As the first year rolls over, the %25 kicks in and the credit card company adds $125 to the bill. That $125 is the interest. At the end of the second year, the %25 is applied again. But, instead of adding $125, the bill goes up $156.25 to $781.25. The jump is because the amount is calculated off the original $500 plus the $125 from the prior year's interest. That's the compounding part. Every time the amount goes up, the calculation includues the amount from all the other times it's risen. The full ten year time line looks like this:
Year Start Interest Total
1 $500.00 $125.00 $625.00
2 $625.00 $156.25 $781.25
3 $781.25 $195.31 $976.56
4 $976.56 $244.14 $1,220.70
5 $1,220.70 $305.18 $1,525.88
6 $1,525.88 $381.47 $1,907.35
7 $1,907.35 $476.84 $2,384.19
8 $2,384.19 $596.05 $2,980.23
9 $2,980.23 $745.06 $3,725.29
10 $3,725.29 $931.32 $4,656.61
## Devilish Details Two things to notice: 1. At year 8, the $596.05 interest charged is more than the initial $500 on the card. 2. Subtracting the initial $500 from the final total shows us that total interest charged is $4,156.61. Four grand that just evaporates out of pocket with out getting anything other than ability to get whatever was purchased for the initial 500 bucks. The pattern is a lot harder to see in the day to day. Monthly payments are made, new charges are added, credit limits are reached. The math is always, though. Working against you as long as you're being charged interest. ## Turning Tables Compound interest is a tool banks and credit card companies use to extract money. Something that's easy to know at one level without realizing how much it actually costs us. == endfilter == endblock
